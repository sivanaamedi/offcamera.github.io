# The Pressure To Always Be Seen

## A reflection about living in a world where attention has become a currency

Today, almost everyone has a camera in their pocket. We can record, edit and share our lives within seconds. Social media has changed the way we communicate, but it has also changed the way we see ourselves.

Many people no longer just experience moments  they think about how these moments will look online. A concert is not only a concert anymore. It can become a video for TikTok. A holiday is not only a memory. It can become a collection of pictures for Instagram. Sometimes it feels like we are not only living our lives, but also performing them for an invisible audience.

The pressure to always be seen is everywhere. Likes, views and followers have become numbers that represent attention. For many people, these numbers can feel like a measurement of success or even personal value. A video with thousands of views can make someone feel important, while a post with little reaction can make someone question themselves.

But why do we need to be seen so badly?

Humans have always searched for connection and recognition. We want to feel accepted and understood. Social media did not create this need, but it created a new place where we constantly search for it. The difference is that today our audience is much bigger. Instead of a small group of friends, we can perform for thousands or even millions of people.

The camera can be powerful. It allows us to express ourselves, share ideas and create art. Many people use social media to inspire others and tell meaningful stories. However, the camera can also create a feeling that we always have to be interesting, beautiful or successful.

Sometimes we forget that the person behind the screen is more important than the image on it.

Living in a world of constant performance can make it difficult to understand who we really are. When we spend so much time creating a version of ourselves for others, we might lose connection with the person we are when nobody is watching.

Maybe the biggest challenge of our generation is learning how to exist without needing constant approval. Not every moment needs to be recorded. Not every experience needs an audience. Some memories are valuable simply because they belong to us.

The camera should be a tool for telling our stories, not a reason to change who we are.

In the end, being seen feels good. It makes us feel connected. But true identity is not created by views, likes or followers. It is created by the moments we experience when the camera is turned off.
